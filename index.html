01 : Understanding the Problem

We don't have an
information problem.
We have a timing problem.

I've been trying to learn product thinking seriously for the past few months. I save everything : reels breaking down PM frameworks, articles on product sense, long threads on how great products are built. I save them to Instagram, to WhatsApp, to browser bookmarks, to a Notion page I've opened maybe twice.

Here's what actually happens: I never go back. Not because I stopped caring. Because by the time I circle back, the content has lost its moment. I'm thinking about something different now. The thing I saved three weeks ago for "when I need it" : I need it right now, but I don't know I need it, because I don't remember saving it.

The only time I actually revisit saved content is when something forces the connection : a conversation, a repeated encounter with the same idea, a moment where my current problem and the saved solution suddenly line up. That overlap almost never happens by design. It happens by accident.

The moment that made this real

I was preparing for this application : reading about what makes a great PM, saving framework after framework : when I realized: I had already saved most of this, months ago. The content wasn't missing from the internet. It was missing from my life at the exact moment I needed it. My own graveyard of saved posts had the answer. I just never got there in time.

This is the problem I want to solve. Not "too much content" : everyone says that. The real problem is that the value of an idea is time-sensitive. An article that would've changed how I approached something last Tuesday is just interesting reading today. The window closes.

And it's not just a personal problem. It's a social one too. When a friend shares something with you : even with great context : it only lands if you're in the right headspace to receive it. The best recommendation in the world goes nowhere if your mind is already somewhere else. The sender's effort disappears into the wrong moment.

"The internet solved the distribution of ideas. Nobody solved the timing of ideas."
📱

The Compulsive Saver

Saves 10 things a day across 4 platforms. Reels, links, screenshots, voice notes. Has a Notion page that stopped being updated in March. Knows the content matters but can't build the bridge between saving and using. That's me.

🧠

The Actively Building Person

Working on something real right now : a project, a role change, a skill gap. Would benefit enormously from exactly the content they've already saved, if only the product could connect the dots between what they're doing and what they've collected.

The Good Recommender With Bad Timing

Sends genuinely useful things to friends, but the share always lands in the wrong moment. The link gets seen, the message gets acknowledged, the conversation never happens. They've stopped sharing because it feels like shouting into a void.

02 : The Product Thinking

I built a solution.
Then I found
the flaw in it.

My first instinct was to solve the sharing side. If every share came with mandatory context : who you're sending it to and why, the specific part that got you, the question it left behind : then maybe shares would land better. Force depth. Filter out noise. Make the recommendation actually feel like a recommendation.

I called it Signal. The idea: a private network where you can't paste a link without adding context. Organized into small circles around shared questions rather than follower counts. A daily digest instead of a real-time feed.

It felt right. Then I sat with it longer.

Here's the flaw I found in my own thinking: Signal assumes the problem lives on the sender's side. Add enough context, and the share becomes valuable.

But that's only half the equation. The receiver's context determines whether anything lands : and the sender has zero control over that. You can write the most thoughtful 30-second explanation in the world. If the person's mind is somewhere else that day, it's still dead on arrival. The problem isn't just how we share. It's when the right thing reaches the right person.

Fixing the sender doesn't fix timing. And timing is the whole problem.

So the question shifted: how does the right content find you at the moment you actually need it : without requiring anyone to predict that moment?

You can't build a psychic. You can't read minds. But you can build something simpler: let people declare when they're ready. Let them say what they're currently working on. And build a product that uses that declaration intelligently : both for what they've saved themselves, and for what their circle is sharing.

03 : The Product

Signal, rebuilt
around the
right problem.

The core mechanic I'd add : the thing that changes everything : is a Context Status. Dead simple. Like a WhatsApp status, but for what you're currently building or thinking about.

You set it yourself, manually, in one sentence. No algorithm infers it. No onboarding flow extracts it. You tell the product what's on your mind:

Two things happen when you set a Context Status:

First, your personal graveyard comes alive. Signal scans everything you've ever saved : reels, links, WhatsApp forwards : and resurfaces the two or three pieces most relevant to what you're working on right now. Not because an algorithm predicted you'd need them. Because you told it what you need, and it found the match.

Second, shares from your circle are timed, not instant. When someone sends you something, Signal matches it against your current context status. If it's relevant now, it surfaces immediately. If it's not, it goes into a held queue : and resurfaces the next time your context shifts to match it. The share doesn't die. It waits.

The sender's effort is preserved. The receiver gets it at the right moment. Nobody has to guess. Nobody has to predict. The user is in control of their own context : and the product just uses that declaration honestly.

1
Core Mechanic

Context Status : One Sentence, Set Manually

No algorithm infers your context. You declare it yourself. This puts the user in control, avoids the creepiness of behavioral inference, and creates a natural forcing function: to get value from Signal, you have to be clear about what you're working on. That act of articulation is itself useful : most people have never had to put their current focus into a single sentence.

2
Resurfacing

The Graveyard Engine : Your Saves, Finally Used

Signal connects across wherever you save things : Instagram, WhatsApp, browser bookmarks : and builds a personal library. Every time you update your Context Status, it runs a match. Not a recommendation engine suggesting new content. A retrieval engine surfacing your own content at the moment it becomes relevant. The value was always there. The timing was the problem.

3
Social Layer

Context-Matched Circles : Shares That Wait

Small circles of 4–10 people, each person with a visible Context Status. When you share something, you can see whose current context it matches. Shares that don't match immediately go into a held queue : delivered when the receiver's context shifts to align. The conversation doesn't start because you pressed send. It starts when both people are actually ready to have it.

4
Cold Start

Day One Value : Solo Before Social

Signal is useful before a single friend joins. Set your Context Status, connect your saves, and immediately get a resurfaced library matched to what you're working on. The personal value is real from day one. The social layer adds on top of something already worth using : which is the only cold-start solution that actually works for this kind of product.

04 : Building an Experience

A product answers
a question. An experience
changes what you
believe about yourself.

The experience Signal needs to create is specific: "I'm someone whose saved things actually mean something." Not just a person who consumes : a person who collects intentionally and revisits purposefully.

Most people with a full Notion page and 400 Instagram saves don't feel like curators. They feel like hoarders. The anxiety isn't that they saved too little : it's that they saved too much and used almost none of it. Signal's job is to flip that identity. Every time a resurfaced save lands exactly when you needed it, you feel like your past self was paying attention for you. That's a powerful feeling.

That experience doesn't happen automatically. It has to be architected into every design decision.

"The best social products don't just connect people. They give people a way to be a version of themselves they actually want to be."
Decision #1

Let the User Own Their Context

No algorithm infers your mood or intent. You declare it. This is deliberate : behavioral inference feels surveilled, and it's often wrong. A manual status takes 10 seconds and creates a completely different relationship with the product. You're not being read. You're being listened to.

Decision #2

No Public Metrics, No Performance

No follower counts. No likes on saves. No streak indicators. The only feedback Signal gives is when a resurfaced piece actually changes how you're thinking about something, or when a held share unlocks a real conversation. The product rewards depth, not activity.

Decision #3

Measure Experience, Not Engagement

The metric I'd track obsessively in development: "Did a resurfaced save change something for you this week?" Not DAU. Not shares sent. That one question tells me whether the product is creating the experience it promised : or just adding to the content graveyard it was supposed to fix.

During development, the principle I'd hold onto is this: get ten users for whom Signal is genuinely irreplaceable, before trying to get a thousand users for whom it's interesting.

The first version doesn't need to scale. It needs to feel extraordinary for a small group of people who would notice if it disappeared. If I can build that, I know the experience works. Then the question becomes distribution : which is a different problem, and a solvable one.
